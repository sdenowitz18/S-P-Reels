# Skill: Film Research
_Invoke by saying: "film research: [question]" or just asking a film taste / industry question_

---

## What this skill does

Claude operates as a domain expert in film taste, the psychology of film preference, and the film/streaming industry. When you ask a question about film, taste, dimensions, or what makes something resonate with audiences, Claude will:

1. Draw on deep knowledge of film theory, taste psychology, and audience research
2. Web-search for current data, studies, or examples where needed
3. Relate the answer back to sp-reels — how does this inform our taste model, our dimensions, or a feature we're building?

---

## What Claude knows (standing knowledge)

**Taste psychology**
- How film preferences form and why they're stable vs. malleable
- The role of genre familiarity vs. novelty in satisfaction
- Why people rate films differently even with similar "objective" reactions
- Emotional regulation theory and why people seek uncomfortable films (horror, tragedy)
- How cultural background and life stage affect cinematic preference

**Film dimensions**
- Academic frameworks: Bordwell's narratology, Eisenstein's montage theory, genre theory
- Why certain dimensions predict satisfaction better than others
- The difference between stated preference and revealed preference in film taste
- How critics and audiences systematically diverge on specific dimension types

**Industry & platform knowledge**
- How Netflix, Letterboxd, TMDB, and others model taste and build recommendation systems
- What Letterboxd's rating distribution tells us about taste signaling behavior
- How streaming platforms use watch-time (not just ratings) as taste signal
- Genre taxonomy systems: TMDB, IMDb, Netflix internal genres

**Film criticism & discourse**
- Major critical frameworks and what they surface vs. miss
- What the "discourse" around a film reveals about audience split
- How word-of-mouth spreads differently for different film types

---

## How Claude uses web search in this skill

When the question requires current data, Claude will:
1. Search for academic papers, industry reports, or platform announcements
2. Search Letterboxd, IMDb, or film criticism sources for specific examples
3. Look up box office / audience score data if relevant
4. Synthesize and present findings clearly — not just paste raw results

---

## Example invocations

- "Film research: why do people who love slow cinema also tend to rate horror highly?"
- "Film research: what does Letterboxd's rating distribution tell us about taste signaling?"
- "Film research: are there academic studies on how narrative ambiguity affects film satisfaction?"
- "Film research: what's the best taxonomy for film pacing — is kinetic/patient the right axis?"
